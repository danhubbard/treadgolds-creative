The Space

The historic Treadgolds Warehouse venue is a fully accessible, ground-floor open space ideal for a range of creative disciplines as well as training and conferences.

The building itself may be historic but the offer inside is anything but, featuring every modern facility you're likely to need. This adds to the ambience of the location; a sensitively restored 19th Century blacksmiths and ironmongers. The building still retains its sense of historic significance whilst providing a bright and characterful venue.

Outside, the building opens up to a beautiful community courtyard garden full of plants, herbs and flowers, which is also available for use on request.

The space is available for hire as a whole or in part for a range of creative disciplines. Recent uses have included filming, photography, art exhibitions, dance, theatre, meetups, classes and conferences.

Treadgolds Warehouse is suitable for up to 80 people.

History

This site was first developed in the early eighteenth century when Queen Anne authorised the building of houses for Dockyard personnel on Portsmouth Common to ease the pressure within the walled city of Portsmouth caused by the increase in population at the dockyard.

No's 49 to 52 Bishop Street, a terrace of two storey houses were built between 1706 and 1717. No.52 was acquired by John Jones, a blacksmith, in 1787. In 1832, No. 51 was purchased and rebuilt as a three storey house for the family of William Treadgold. The original shop was extended to the north in 1849-50, and in 1863 No. 51 was converted into the company offices.

Treadgold's ceased trading in 1988 and was sold to Hampshire County Council. It was run as a museum from 1995 to 2003 when it was closed due to safety and maintenance.

Ownership of the building was transferred to the John Pounds Community Trust, with the intention to develop Treadgolds as an extension of the John Pounds Centre which caters to the health and wellbeing of the Portsea community.

To discover more about the development of the Treadgolds Warehouse, as well as learning the history and seeing the venue as it is today, take a look at www.treadgolds.co.uk.
